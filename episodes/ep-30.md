Three days after it launched, the best model in the world disappeared: a US national-security export-control directive suspended all access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 by any foreign national — inside or outside the country, including Anthropic's own foreign-national employees — and Anthropic cut access entirely within 90 minutes, reportedly after an AWS security team flagged a jailbreak where the "scary" prompt was just "fix this code." Shimin, Dan, and Rahul read it as the end of the universal-access era, then move through Meta's applied-AI "gulag" (laid-off engineers drafted to write puzzles for weaker models on full salary plus RSUs), the Elias Thorne "dataset virus" that propagated a fictional lighthouse keeper across every frontier model via shared training data, howfastis.ai on why AI's six-month doubling can't outrun a 2%/yr economy, Addy Osmani's loop engineering (the six pieces of a real agent harness), Dan's local DeepSeek 4 Flash demo on a Framework desktop, and a deep dive into Christopher Koch's paper arguing AI doesn't steepen the Dunning-Kruger curve — it shatters it. ## Takeaways

- **The export ban may have ended the universal-access era for frontier models.** Three days after launch, a national-security export-control order suspended Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for *all* foreign nationals — even Anthropic's own — and since the system had no way to enforce that, Anthropic just shut the whole thing off in ~90 minutes (planting "unavailable" notices across clients) and flew staff to DC to lobby (Axios). A security researcher claims it traces to an AWS team that found a jailbreak where the alarming prompt was merely "fix this code." The fallout the hosts care about: developers now want to self-host because access can be yanked overnight, and non-US teams say they can't trust American inference providers that a government can switch off. Shimin recounts his closest brush yet with "AI psychosis" using Fable 5 to design a novel (non-quadcopter) drone — a sample size of one, but a real step up he'd pay API prices for on research synthesis, if not day-to-day coding.
- **Meta's "AI gulag" is a morale story, not a labor-camp one.** Alexandr Wang's applied-AI unit (he ran Scale AI before joining Meta) has been drafting laid-off engineers to write puzzles, coding problems, and basic data labels to train Meta's weaker models — the rationale being that Meta engineers beat Scale's contractors. Internal calls reportedly boiled over with expletives (TechCrunch). The hosts push back on the "gulag" framing — these are full salaries plus vesting RSUs — but flag the real issues: getting an "applied AI" transfer and discovering it's leetcode-for-models is demoralizing, and your keystrokes are almost certainly being logged as training signal. Rahul's prediction: the tell-all "the gulag was so bad I only made a few million dollars" memoirs are coming.
- **The Elias Thorne dataset virus is a preview of training-data poisoning.** Per 404 Media, Cornell's Sil Hamilton and David Mimno sampled ~20,000 stories from ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini using just five prompts and found ~88% feature one of a few recurring characters — most notably "Elias Thorne," a lighthouse keeper. The trail leads to ~111 conversations in a public ChatGPT-3.5 dataset that got reused to train later models across every lab, so the character spread virally and now even "authors" books on Amazon. The unsettling implication: if an innocent artifact can cross-contaminate every frontier model through shared training data, a deliberate poisoning campaign could too — and we might not notice. (Great tangent on LLM vocabulary tells: "substrate," "hedge," and the now-ubiquitous "why this matters.")
- **AI is fast; the economy is a weak-link system.** Rahul's pick, howfastis.ai (Emory Taziki), pits AI's ~6-month doubling of task-completion horizons against 150 years of ~2%/yr economic growth. Leaning on Chad Jones's weak-link theory and Goldratt's Theory of Constraints, the argument is that the bottleneck sets the pace — and the bottleneck is human judgment, accountability, ambiguous requirements, and owning the outcome when it breaks. A counterintuitive data point: computing's share of US GDP actually *fell* (4.3% in 2000 → ~3% by 2023) as prices dropped, so "intelligence everywhere" may not move GDP — and per-token cost may follow the same deflation over 15–20 years. The devs' version: system design and messy-data integration might get automated, but ambiguous requirements and outcome ownership won't, and "residual decision rights" plus cognitive debt keep a human on the hook (maybe solved by insurance — though self-driving still hasn't settled liability after 20 years).
- **Loop engineering: the six pieces that turn a bare /loop into a real harness.** Shimin's pick, Addy Osmani's "Loop Engineering," extends the Ralph loop (slash-loop an agent until a goal is reached) with six components: scheduled automations (the "heartbeat"), worktrees (so parallel agents don't collide — the industry consolidated here after early Gas Town used multiple repo copies), skills (write down project knowledge to fight hallucination and thrashing), plugins/connectors (reach beyond the filesystem), subagents (the key move: split the agent that *does* the work from the one that *reviews* it), and a memory store. Both Codex and Claude Code already are full loop-engineering harnesses. Shimin's throughline: prompt engineering → spec-driven development → loop engineering, where you keep the goal looser but bake judgment into reusable skills — so the loop *amplifies* your judgment. The metaphor: handsaw → table saw ("watch your fingers") → tree-harvesting heavy machinery. The drawbacks Osmani names: verification still falls on the developer, and cognitive debt bites when it breaks at 3am.
- **AI doesn't steepen Dunning-Kruger — it decouples competence from self-assessment.** The deep dive on Christopher Koch's "Beyond the Steeper Curve" argues AI-mediated *metacognitive decoupling* breaks the link between output quality and understanding: in an LSAT-logic study, AI raised everyone's scores *and* everyone overrated themselves, flattening the classic curve — observable output and self-assessment shoot up while real understanding barely moves and calibration stagnates or deteriorates. Shimin: "it gave everybody Dunning-Kruger." The companion piece (No One's Happy, "Appearing Productive in the Workplace") names the organizational failure mode: when productivity stops signaling competence, the "slop grenade" — a polished, subtly-wrong AI artifact lobbed at a colleague — weaponizes cheap AI to consume expensive expert attention. That attention asymmetry, plus the sycophancy trap and management's preference for the *appearance* of momentum, is what's corrosive. Shimin's proposed punishment: make the sender review their own slop grenade scaled up to a slop A-bomb.
- **Local frontier-ish models at home, and a Two Minutes to Midnight that's still on watch.** Dan demoed DeepSeek V4 Flash ("DS4," the "dwarf star runner") running locally on a Framework desktop (Ryzen 395 Max, 128GB unified memory) over ROCm at ~14 tok/s, wired to the Pi agent on a ~$4,000 box with no cloud — the demo gloriously self-derailed into a heartbeat task and a Playwright browser launch to look up "slop grenade." Then the bubble check: a Claude SDK now runs as an Apple Foundation Model (a commoditization tell — Apple already uses Gemini for Siri, so "no durable moat"), subsidized inference is ending (Microsoft pulling back; TechCrunch's GLM-5.1 + Opus cost-splitting test), and an NBER paper (Walter & Walter) prices genuine hyperscaler insolvency risk: ~$381B in 2025 capex heading past $1T by 2027, ~20% of US private fixed investment, with ~3.4% of GDP effectively bet on the singularity. With SpaceX up ~25% post-IPO and the market shrugging off the warning flags, the clock moves from 5:20 back to **5:30**.
