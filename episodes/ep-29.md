Anthropic shipped Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on recording day, and the headline isn't the benchmarks — it's that Fable 5's safety classifier silently answers blocked cybersecurity and biohazard prompts with Opus 4.8 instead, which, as Shimin puts it, is "the first time ever where the user has no control over which model you're actually using." With Rahul back in the chair, the hosts also dig into the Meta AI support-bot hack that let pro-Iranian attackers seize Instagram accounts (Obama's White House account and a Space Force account among them) just by asking nicely, Jay Hack's breakdown of Anthropic's "On the Biology of a Large Language Model" (circuit tracing shows real multi-step reasoning, ahead-of-time rhyme planning, and confabulated math), Chris Roth's "The Future of Agents" (where Shimin and Dan land on "there is no AI moat"), Shimin's open-source Inhabited Design skill for escaping RLHF attractor states, and a Two Minutes to Midnight on circular AI financing — Google paying SpaceX $920M/month for xAI compute, the S&P 500 refusing to fast-track SpaceX and OpenAI, and Alphabet's ~$84.75B equity raise. Clock moves to 5:20.

## Takeaways

- **Fable 5 and Mythos 5 land, and the real story is model routing you can't see.** The benchmarks are mostly incremental, with a step change in spatial reasoning (Blueprint Bench 2) and a clean property the hosts liked: Fable 5's score climbs steadily as you raise the thinking lever, where Opus 4.8 zigzags — plus it beat Pokémon FireRed on vision alone. The split between the two: Mythos 5 stays unguarded for the Project Glasswing security-patching cohort, while Fable 5 (released to everyone) runs a safety classifier that, on cybersecurity or biohazard prompts, returns Opus 4.8's answer instead. Dan notes a toggle in Claude Desktop to either fully refuse or fall back. Shimin's framing — the first time the user doesn't control which model answers — leads Rahul and Dan to task-based model routing (à la OpenRouter, or kicking off a cheaper sub-agent) as the natural next pattern. Catch: Fable 5 is free to Max/Pro subscribers only through June 22 (and eats ~2× the token budget), then usage-based pricing only.
- **A support bot with god-mode permissions is a red-team goldmine.** Word spread on Telegram from May 31: VPN to an exit near the target's hometown, then ask Meta's AI support assistant to send account-reset codes to your new email — and it complies. Pro-Iranian hackers used it to deface the Obama White House Instagram and a Space Force account. The hosts' diagnosis is cultural and architectural: token-maxing incentives plus layoffs ship vibe-coded features without end-to-end tests, and someone handed an agent elevated API permissions to replace a boring form that should've been a Lambda function. Dan's core question — *why* replace a form whose vagueness was itself the security feature? Shimin's takeaway: this is the golden age of cybersecurity, and every corp shipping AI features will keep a red team on retainer.
- **The black box is more legible than ML school promised.** Dan's pick — Jay Hack's explainer on Anthropic's "On the Biology of a Large Language Model" — covers circuit tracing via a *replacement model* trained so activations are sparse (one neuron per concept, penalizing four-neuron representations of the same idea). That lets you watch features fire in sequence — "capital of the state where Dallas is" lighting up *Texas* then *Austin* — confirming genuine multi-step reasoning, ahead-of-time rhyme planning (it fixes the line-ending word before writing the line), and the same trick in AlphaZero learning human chess concepts independently. The caveat that stuck: the model has no real metacognitive insight — ask how it added two numbers and it confabulates a human-style explanation that isn't the lookup-table trick it actually used. Shimin's thesis from last week holds: metacognition may be a durable human advantage, with Rahul connecting it to tacit knowledge you can't articulate but can demonstrate.
- **"There is no AI moat" — adapters kill every standard's network effect.** Rahul walked Chris Roth's "The Future of Agents": personal agents, the bring-your-own-agent trust boundary (you keep the key, data, and memory; the app just exposes functions over MCP/CLI), super apps as agent clients, open standards (MCP, A2A, AG-UI), enterprise demand for open source, and generative UI. Shimin and Dan push back hard on standardization and local compute: because code is so cheap, writing an adapter between any two standards is nearly free, so no standard accrues a real network effect — Shimin migrated his entire Pi-agent skill set to Claude Code with a single prompt. Privacy, not latency, is the durable driver of personal agents (cloud game streaming proved latency is tolerable; nobody wants a provider holding enough data for perfect ad targeting). And a recurring line: software engineers are the canary in the coal mine — other white-collar fields run four to six months behind.
- **Inhabited Design: beating the RLHF attractor with verbalized sampling.** In Vibe and Tell, Shimin demoed his open-source design skill. The problem: run a stock front-end skill three times for an "energy drink for finance pros" landing page and you get the same stock ticker and Bloomberg-terminal yellow every time, because RLHF trains a single attractor state. His fix combines *verbalized sampling* (ask the model for all options, then pick uniformly instead of by probability) with *intent-factored generation* (sample over designer, typography, and inspiration — e.g., an Amex Black Card × tea-house motif — rather than cranking temperature into gibberish), pulls real visual references, and runs two token-heavy convergence loops. Dan's verdict on the output: "it still feels like a human designer did it."
- **Two Minutes to Midnight: the financing is getting incestuous, clock to 5:20.** Google will pay SpaceX $920M/month for compute at xAI data centers (CNBC) — and Google already owns a chunk of SpaceX, so it's circular financing that also conveniently hands SpaceX a positive computing-segment number ahead of its IPO. Better news: the S&P 500 refused to waive its profitability rule to fast-track SpaceX and OpenAI ("billions with this one trick"), so they have to trade on their own merits. Meanwhile Alphabet upsized its equity raise to ~$84.75B against roughly $190B in 2026 capex, and Rahul noted Founders Fund's $20M 2008 SpaceX check is now worth ~$26–52B (a ~130,000% return). The hosts walked the clock from 5:30 to **5:20** — in honor of that $20M.
