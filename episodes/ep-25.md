Why is Anthropic now running Claude on Elon Musk's GPUs — right after Elon sued OpenAI for the same kind of nonprofit-to-for-profit hack? Why does the road to a billion-token context window require breaking the GPU and memory apart entirely? And what does it mean when even Simon Willison reluctantly admits he ships code he hasn't read and runs Claude Code with `--dangerously-skip-permissions` by default? Shimin, Dan, and Rahul cover Brockman's leaked deposition journal (with the Ilya-commissioned Tesla painting Musk stormed out clutching, and the Model 3 co-founder bribes), the Anthropic-SpaceX/XAI deal for access to Colossus One's 220,000 NVIDIA GPUs, NVIDIA's Rubin CPX disaggregation architecture and the memory bandwidth wall, Phil Schmid's four sub-agent patterns for 2026 (inline / fan-out / agent pool / agent teams), Jack Clark on automated AI research at 60% by 2028 and the alignment math that makes 0.1% misalignment compound to 60% accuracy in 500 generations, Simon Willison on review fatigue and the normalization of deviance, Dexter Horthy publicly recanting his dark-factory agentic-engineering stance, James Shore on why a productivity doubling without inverse maintenance gains hits a velocity wall within 12 months, and the Wall Street Journal on Grok's collapse to "RC Cola" status — 20M to 8M downloads, 0.174% paid penetration vs 6% for ChatGPT.

## Takeaways

- Brockman's deposition journal is the kind of detail the legal argument can't compete with: Musk demanded full control of OpenAI, allegedly bribed co-founders with free Model 3s, was given an Ilya-commissioned Tesla painting that he stormed out of the meeting still holding. The receipts make OpenAI look less like the bad guys — even though the underlying nonprofit-to-for-profit conversion grievance is real and even Elon's haters concede the point.
- Anthropic's compute crunch ended where its enemies live: access to SpaceX/XAI's Colossus One (220,000 NVIDIA GPUs), lifted Pro/Max token caps, removed Claude Code peak-hour limits, and expressed interest in SpaceX orbital data centers. The shape of the deal makes XAI's standalone viability look weak — Elon's pragmatism overrides the lawsuit when green money shows up — and Anthropic now has commitments to Microsoft, Google, Amazon, NVIDIA, and SpaceX in a single quarter.
- NVIDIA's Rubin CPX treats memory as the real inference bottleneck. Disaggregation decouples memory modules from the GPU and pre-fills via the network stack, fighting the bandwidth wall behind the 60% quality drop near context limits. The shift from training-heavy to inference-heavy compute reframes what GPUs are even for — memory matters more than FLOPs for serving — and the RAMpocalypse is forecast to last another year while new fabs come online.
- Phil Schmid's four sub-agent patterns ladder up in evaluation difficulty: inline (function call) → fan-out (map-reduce) → agent pool (persistent + coordinator) → agent teams (direct/mailbox comms). Each step adds state and dynamism, which makes testing exponentially harder. Rahul reframes it: agent patterns are reorgs without HR overhead, and the next layer is a meta-agent that picks the pattern per task.
- Jack Clark puts automated AI research at 60% probability by 2028. SWE-Bench is effectively saturated at 94%, METR's task horizon hits 12 hours for Opus 4.6, CORE-Bench at 95.5%. The alignment math is the brake: 0.1% misalignment compounds across self-reinforcing loops to ~60% accuracy in 500 generations — and a non-trivial chunk of misalignment training data is fiction about pre-LLM AIs ("I'm sorry I can't do that, Dave" is literally in the corpus).
- Simon Willison's reluctant normalization of vibe coding is the first real recantation from a credible skeptic — he now ships code he hasn't read, runs Claude Code with `--dangerously-skip-permissions` by default, and frames it as the same trust-in-APIs problem you have with any large team. Pair with Dexter Horthy publicly walking back his dark-factory stance at AI Engineer Europe and the agentic-engineering hype cycle is meeting its first round of disillusionment from its biggest proponents.
- James Shore's math is brutal and underdiscussed: if codegen productivity doubles but maintenance hours stay constant, maintenance hits 50% of your time within ~12 months. Long-running agents fit dependency upgrades and supply-chain patches, but still pick poor abstractions and double down on them — so refactoring trust lags coding trust, and the next velocity wall is maintainability, not codegen.
- The bubble clock moves further from midnight (4 → 6 minutes). $2T revenue backlog across Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and Oracle is approaching 1990s national-debt scale, but no major red flags landed this week and Anthropic's compute crunch resolved. Grok's collapse — 20M → 8M downloads, 0.174% paid vs ChatGPT's 6% — is the cleanest signal of a market with a real loser: "OpenAI is Coke, Anthropic is Pepsi, Grok is RC Cola."
