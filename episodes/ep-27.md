Sam Altman beat Elon Musk in court — and a long New Yorker piece argues we all lost, because the real question under the verdict is whether any one person should own AI safety. Rahul's out this week, so Shimin and Dan break down the OpenAI–Musk trial (OpenAI won on a statute-of-limitations technicality; Musk's attorney argued "we could all die" from AI while the judge noted he'd mean it more if he didn't fund xAI; the courtroom "butt pillows" become the running complacency metaphor, alongside Anthropic's own admission that market forces are dragging it from safety-first into profit-seeking), Google's Gemini 3.5 Flash shipping Flash-only straight into AI Overviews because it's cheap and fast enough to run for billions of searches (Dan's bet: it's on TPUs) — mathier than 3.1 but returning fewer results, the viral "can't search 'disregard'" outage a harness failure not a model one, and a pelican dressed for a Miami crypto conference, a Hardware Hut on rosmine's $48K six-by-RTX-6000-Ada server that breaks even near 75–85% utilization then runs at ~$125/month and constant riser failures (versus Shimin's 128 GB-Mac-or-keep-paying-Anthropic version of the question), a Technique Corner on AI-assisted-engineer burnout from Evil Martians and Siddhant Khare — cap parallel agents at 3–4, keep hands on the keyboard, accept 70% and hand-code the rest — capped by Microsoft cancelling Claude Code subscriptions after costs topped human developers, Borretti's "Human Bottlenecks" and why the $100M startup in your laptop stays there, Dan's rant on the "slop grenade" (the nohello.com successor), and a Two Minutes to Midnight on the SpaceX/OpenAI/Anthropic IPO index squeeze and isaiprofitable.com's capex carnage — clock eases back to 6:15.

## Takeaways

- OpenAI beat Elon Musk on a statute-of-limitations technicality, but Dan's read of the long New Yorker piece is that the actual story is two billionaires fighting over who controls a for-profit AI company — with the subtext that Musk was mostly angry he didn't think of it first. The real question underneath: should individuals own AI safety at all, or does OpenAI's nonprofit-to-for-profit structure make the quest for profit structurally inevitable? Anthropic published a parallel admission this week that market forces are forcing it from safety-first into also-make-money. Best pull quote: Musk's attorney Stephen Molo argued "we could all die" from AI, and the judge observed he might mean it more sincerely if he weren't funding xAI. The recurring "butt pillow" — OpenAI's lawyers brought their own seat cushions to a hard-benched courtroom — becomes the metaphor for collective complacency. Actions over words.
- Google shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash at I/O Flash-only and dropped it straight into AI Overviews — meaning it's cheap and fast enough to run for billions of free web searches (Dan's hypothesis: "Flash" effectively means it runs on a TPU). On Shimin's unscientific "what do I do with 2.5 acres in Washington" Deep Search benchmark it's more analytical and "mathy" than 3.1, leaning on LaTeX, but returns half a dozen well-summarized options instead of 20–25 — fewer, less diverse results. The viral few-minutes outage where you couldn't search "disregard" (or "cancel"/"abort") was a harness failure, not a model failure — they needed more regex guards. And it drew a pelican that Simon Willison and Hacker News pegged as dressed for a Miami crypto conference. Takeaway: Google is not out of the race; this is a frontier-ish model they have the spare compute to give away.
- Hardware Hut: rosmine, an ex-FAANG researcher who left in 2024 to do AI research, built a six-by-RTX-6000-Ada server (~$48K) after the Ada won on price-to-throughput versus H100/A100 — and hired a pro to wire it because the rig needs roughly a 40-amp line and could otherwise set the house on fire. The verdict: at ~75–85% utilization it hits break-even, then costs ~$125/month in electricity, punctuated by a utilization graph full of dips that are mostly "server broke" and "riser failed." The catch is that 80%-plus utilization is unrealistic for anyone not running RL experiments. Shimin's consumer version — a 128 GB unified-memory Mac for local models versus just paying Anthropic — pencils out as a multi-year payback even at the $200 tier, hard to justify unless you're toying with weights or you care about data privacy (Dan's local-journal use case).
- Two burnout posts in one Technique Corner — Evil Martians' "AI-Assisted Engineers Are Burning Out" and Siddhant Khare's "AI Fatigue Is Real." The mitigations: acknowledge your wins (you still shape the output distribution), rethink your workflow to cut context-switching, cap parallel agents at 3–4, keep exercising the craft with hands on keyboard, time-box AI sessions, separate AI-time from thinking-time, and accept ~70% from AI and hand-code the rest. Dan's addition: the posts skip the biggest cause — value mismatch, where you still grieve the craft while leaderboards and the org reward raw token throughput. Shimin's confession: seven concurrent Claude Code sessions after work. The capper — Microsoft cancelled Claude Code subscriptions for many staff after costs exceeded human developers, the token-count pendulum visibly swinging back like blockchain-on-everything settling to a few real use cases.
- "Human Bottlenecks" (borretti.me) pushes back on the viral "there's a $100M startup in your laptop, you just need the right sequence of words" tweet. Two reasons it stays in your laptop: most people lack a serious context of use (building a flashcard app when you don't use flashcards), and the limiter was never the tools — it's you, your energy, judgment, motivation, and executive function. Shimin's counter: a sufficiently general intelligence may coax that capability out of most people anyway, and he's openly betting the internal-limit hypothesis is wrong. The segment spirals into the first AI-native generation (Dan's encounter with a six-year-old giggling at ChatGPT), the observation that "digital native" didn't actually raise software competency, and a half-serious future where we visit Enlightenment cafés to keep our judgment sharp the way we now pay to do manual labor at the gym.
- Dan's Rant: the "slop grenade" (noslopgrenade.com) — pasting raw Claude output at a coworker instead of an actual answer, framed as the spiritual successor to nohello.com. The etiquette didn't change: lead with your one-line take plus a couple lines of reasoning, then attach the full output for anyone who wants the kaboom — and ideally flag that it's AI-generated. Bonus failure mode is "let me Claude that for you" energy, where the asker could have pointed Claude at the repo themselves. Conclusion: don't lob a dictionary three cubicles over when someone asks what a word means.
- Two Minutes to Midnight: Zachary Evans' Morningstar piece shows the Morningstar 100 is already >50% concentrated in eight tech names, and the coming SpaceX (~4.5%), OpenAI (~3%), and Anthropic (~2.5%) IPOs could add ~10% more — with Nasdaq cutting its post-IPO wait from 12 months to ~15 trading days and the S&P weighing the same fast-entry change, funneling index-fund and 401(k) money in from day one. Meanwhile isaiprofitable.com (fresh off Hacker News) answers its title with a resounding no: Amazon leads AI capex at −$291B, Alphabet −$262B, Microsoft −$235B, with OpenAI (−$27B) and Anthropic (−$26B) almost a rounding error, and only Nvidia in the green at +$253B. With the rule changes set to mitigate the cash crunch, the clock eases back to 6:15.
