# The complete podcast growth playbook for ADI

**ADI can realistically grow from 100 to 500+ downloads per episode within 90 days by focusing on three high-leverage activities: repurposing each episode into 15+ content pieces, systematic cross-promotion with similar AI/dev podcasts, and building a weekly newsletter as a listener retention engine.** The good news: nearly everything that works at this stage is free. The $50/month budget is best saved for periodic Overcast ad buys rather than spread thin across social ads. What follows is a section-by-section blueprint with exact tools, pricing, templates, and timelines — all calibrated for a three-person engineering team with limited marketing time and a developer-first audience.

## 1. Turn every episode into 15+ content pieces

The single biggest unlock for a small podcast is **content multiplication** — transforming one weekly recording into clips, posts, articles, and newsletters that reach audiences across every platform. Here's the exact workflow and tool stack.

**Recommended tool stack ($34/month):**

| Tool | Cost | What it does |
|------|------|-------------|
| **Castmagic** | $19/mo (Hobby, 300 min) | Generates transcripts, show notes, blog posts, social posts, newsletter drafts, and Twitter threads from audio |
| **OpusClip** | $15/mo (Starter, 150 min) | AI-identifies best clip moments, auto-reframes to vertical, adds animated captions, exports for all platforms |
| **Headliner** | Free (5 unwatermarked/mo) | Creates audiogram clips with waveform animations |
| **Canva** | Free | Thumbnails, quote cards, carousel graphics |
| **Claude or ChatGPT** | Free tier | Additional content generation, rephrasing, thread writing |

**Optimal clip lengths by platform:**

| Platform | Sweet spot | Maximum | Notes |
|----------|-----------|---------|-------|
| YouTube Shorts | **50–58 seconds** | 3 minutes | Educational "one insight" clips perform best |
| LinkedIn video | **60–90 seconds** | 10 minutes | Insight-driven, professional tone — ADI's highest-value platform |
| Twitter/X video | **15–45 seconds** | 2 min 20 sec | Punchy single-point clips; hot takes on AI news |
| TikTok | **24–38 seconds** (viral) / 60–90 sec (educational) | 10 minutes | Completion rate is the algorithm's top signal |
| Instagram Reels | **15–30 seconds** | 3 minutes | Sharp drop-off after 60 seconds |

**Weekly workflow for three hosts (3–4 hours total):**

**Host A (Producer):** Upload episode audio to Castmagic and video to OpusClip. Review AI-generated outputs, approve or lightly edit. Time: ~45 min.

**Host B (Content):** Take Castmagic's blog draft and newsletter draft, polish both, write the YouTube description with SEO keywords, schedule social posts via Buffer or Typefully. Time: ~60 min.

**Host C (Visual/Distribution):** Create one thumbnail in Canva, select the 5 best OpusClip clips, post Shorts and Reels, create 1–2 Headliner audiograms. Time: ~60 min.

**Per-episode output target:** 1 full YouTube episode with chapters, **5–10 short-form clips** across platforms, 1 blog post, 1 newsletter edition, 5+ social media posts (LinkedIn + Twitter), 1–2 audiograms, complete transcript and show notes. This approach compounds — each piece of content becomes a discovery surface that drives listeners back to the podcast.

## 2. YouTube is now the #1 podcast discovery platform

**39% of monthly U.S. podcast consumers** discover shows on YouTube — more than any other platform. For a tech podcast, YouTube isn't optional; it's the primary growth channel. Here's how to optimize.

**Titles that work for developer audiences:** Place the primary keyword at the start, keep under 60 characters, and follow the formula "Main Keyword + Benefit + Hook." Good example: *"Why RAG Is Replacing Fine-Tuning — 5 Patterns That Work"*. Bad example: *"Episode 42: We Talk About Stuff."* Include the guest's name at the end if they're notable. Study channels like Fireship (punchy, opinionated titles) and Dwarkesh Patel (topic + guest name format) for inspiration.

**Thumbnails:** Use **0–3 words of bold text**, a close-up face showing strong emotion (increases CTR by 20–30%), and high-contrast colors. Create 2–3 reusable templates in Canva Free — swap the text and photo each week. Consistency in thumbnail style builds brand recognition as viewers scroll. Install **VidIQ** (free tier) to preview how thumbnails appear at different sizes and research competitor keywords.

**Shorts strategy for growth:** Post **3–5 Shorts per week** cut from each long-form episode. Target 50–58 seconds of one clear insight with a hook in the first 2 seconds. End each Short with a verbal CTA: "Full breakdown in the episode — link in bio." The Shorts algorithm rapidly tests content across new audiences, and channels maintaining consistent Shorts schedules see **67% faster subscriber growth**. Use OpusClip's Virality Score to identify which moments will perform best.

**YouTube SEO checklist per upload:** Include the keyword in the title and first two lines of the description. Write **200+ word descriptions** with timestamps (format: `0:00 Introduction`). Add 2–5 relevant hashtags. Upload accurate captions/subtitles. Add cards at natural topic transitions linking to related episodes. Set up end screens in the last 20 seconds with a subscribe button and "next episode" suggestion. Create playlists by topic ("AI Agent Deep Dives," "LLM Paper Breakdowns") — playlists signal to YouTube that your channel has organized, bingeable content.

**Free tools needed:** VidIQ (keyword research, thumbnail preview), YouTube Studio analytics, Canva Free (thumbnails), YouTube's built-in chapter and end screen features.

## 3. Where developer audiences actually hang out — and how to show up

Developer communities are the most effective free distribution channel for ADI, but each community has strict norms around self-promotion. The key principle: **contribute value for 2–4 weeks before ever mentioning the podcast**, and when you do, lead with the insight, not the link.

**Reddit (highest potential, strictest rules):**

**r/LocalLLaMA** (~500K members) is the best fit — this community values practical experiments, benchmarks, and hands-on tutorials. If an episode covers local LLM benchmarks or model comparisons, write a text post with key findings and mention the podcast as supplementary material at the bottom. **r/MachineLearning** (~3M members) is strictly moderated; participate in paper discussions for weeks, then occasionally reference "we discussed this on our podcast" in relevant comment threads. **r/SideProject** and **r/shamelessplug** explicitly welcome self-promotion and are the right places for a direct "we built this podcast" introduction post. For all subreddits, follow the **9:1 ratio** — nine genuine contributions for every one self-promotional post. Build 100–500 karma before attempting to share podcast content in stricter communities.

**Hacker News (highest ceiling, hardest to crack):** Don't post bare episode links. Instead, write a companion blog post with technical takeaways and submit that. Use the **"Show HN"** format for a launch post: *"Show HN: Three engineers break down AI papers with actual code."* The ideal HN post title is clear, descriptive, and free of marketing language. Respond to every comment candidly. Also watch for recurring **"Ask HN: What podcasts do you listen to?"** threads — these appear periodically and generate massive engagement.

**Dev.to and Hashnode:** Transform each episode into a cross-posted article with code examples and an embedded player. Set canonical URLs pointing to adipod.ai to avoid SEO cannibalization. Posts with code snippets and diagrams achieve **2x higher engagement** than text-only content.

**Discord servers to join immediately:** OpenAI Discord, Anthropic Discord, Hugging Face Discord, Learn AI Together (~93K members), and the MLOps Community Discord. Lurk for 1–2 weeks, answer questions, share resources — then naturally mention relevant episodes when they genuinely help someone.

**Slack communities (must-join):** The **MLOps Community** (~28K in Slack) and **DataTalks.Club** (~80K members) are the two highest-signal communities. Both have channels for sharing content and resources. Join, participate in discussions for 2+ weeks, then share episode insights (not just links) in relevant topic channels.

## 4. SEO turns episodes into evergreen discovery machines

Podcast audio is invisible to Google. But transcripts, blog posts, and optimized episode pages turn every episode into a long-tail search asset that drives listeners for months.

**Build a proper website (don't rely on Transistor's built-in site):** Transistor's default site has no blog functionality and limited SEO control. The fastest upgrade is **Podpage** ($15/mo) — it auto-imports episodes from your Transistor RSS feed, adds PodcastSeries and PodcastEpisode schema markup automatically, creates transcript pages, and is SEO-optimized out of the box. For maximum flexibility, use WordPress with the **Rank Math** plugin (free, with a Pro tier at $59/year that includes a dedicated Podcast Schema module).

**Essential pages to build:** Individual episode pages (the core SEO asset — each functions as a search landing page), topic hub pages ("AI Coding Tools," "LLM Research," "Multi-Agent Systems") that cluster related episodes, a resources page linking tools mentioned across episodes, and an about page with host credentials.

**Transcripts are the single biggest SEO multiplier.** Use **OpenAI Whisper API** ($0.006/min ≈ $0.30 per 50-minute episode) or **MacWhisper** (free basic / $79 one-time for Pro) to generate transcripts. Publish them on each episode page — collapsible or in a separate tab. Transcripts make your audio content crawlable and unlock long-tail keyword visibility.

**Keyword strategy for the AI-for-developers niche:** Target long-tail keywords where a small site can actually rank. High-opportunity examples include "multi-agent orchestration framework tutorial," "spec-driven development guide," "Claude vs GPT for coding 2026," "MCP server setup guide," and "AI code review tools comparison." Use **Google Keyword Planner** (free) and **Ubersuggest** (3 free searches/day) for volume and competition data. Turn each episode topic into a 1,500–2,500 word blog post targeting a specific keyword.

**Show notes structure for maximum SEO value:** Use keyword-rich URLs (`adipod.ai/episodes/ai-coding-tools-comparison-2026`), place the primary keyword near the beginning of the title, write a 150–300 word summary with natural keyword usage, include 3–5 key takeaways, add timestamps, embed the player (increases dwell time — a Google ranking factor), publish the full transcript, link to resources mentioned, and cross-link related episodes.

**Platform-specific SEO:** On Apple Podcasts, include primary keywords in the podcast title and description — Apple indexes podcast name, episode titles, and author tag. Expand episode descriptions to **400+ words**. On Spotify, behavioral signals like completion rate, saves, and shares drive discoverability. On YouTube, upload transcripts as captions and write keyword-optimized descriptions with timestamps. Set up **Google Search Console** and **Google Analytics 4** (both free) immediately to track progress.

## 5. Cross-promotion is the highest-ROI growth tactic at this stage

When you advertise on another podcast, **100% of listeners are already podcast consumers** — the biggest friction barrier disappears. The average listener subscribes to 3–5 shows, so you're not stealing audiences; you're expanding reach. One podcaster documented going from **100 downloads/week to 1,000** during a four-week host-read promo campaign.

**Five cross-promotion methods (ranked by effectiveness):**

- **Host-read promos** — The partner host personally recommends ADI in their own voice. Most effective due to trust transfer; 54% of listeners are more likely to consider recommendations heard in podcasts (Edison Research)
- **Trailer/promo swaps** — Exchange 30–60 second pre-recorded spots. Easiest to execute and the best starting point
- **Guest swaps** — You appear on their show, they appear on yours. Deepest content collaboration
- **Feed drops** — Drop a full episode into a partner's feed with their intro. Best during partner's hiatus
- **Joint episodes** — Co-create content together. Each publishes to their own feed

**Best cross-promotion targets for ADI:** Last Week in AI (similar news-roundup format), Practical AI (practical ML guidance with banter), AI for Humans (humor + AI news — strong tone match), AI Native Dev (weekly AI + software development), The Cognitive Revolution (AI builders focus), Machine Learning Street Talk (technical paper discussions), High Agency (LLM product builders), Data Skeptic, Software Engineering Daily, and CoRecursive. Start with shows at a similar size (within 2–3x of your downloads) — they're most likely to agree.

**Where to find partners:** **MatchMaker.fm** (70K+ members, free, filter by niche and audience size) is the best starting point. Also use **Podchaser** (free discovery, "IMDb of podcasting"), **PodMatch** (matching algorithm for hosts and guests), and **PodcastGuests.com** (free newsletter connecting guests and hosts with 47K+ experts).

**Guest booking strategy:** The guest types that drive the most new listeners are (1) other podcasters in your niche (they'll promote the episode to their audience), (2) open-source maintainers of popular AI tools like LangChain or Ollama (built-in developer community), (3) dev tool founders (motivated to promote, their audience matches yours), and (4) AI researchers with upcoming publications (their PR teams actively seek podcast appearances). Find guests on GitHub Trending, Twitter/X, conference speaker lists, and Hacker News. Create a **"Guest Kit"** for each episode — audiogram clips, quote graphics, pre-written social posts — and send it in a follow-up email on launch day. Making it effortless for guests to share is the key.

**Each host should also appear as a guest on 2–3 other podcasts per month.** Develop a unique "guest topic" per host, create a one-page media kit with bio, photo, and 3–5 topic ideas, and always end guest appearances with a specific CTA: "Search 'Artificial Developer Intelligence' wherever you listen."

## 6. LinkedIn and Twitter are ADI's two highest-ROI social platforms

**LinkedIn strategy (B2B professionals):**

The critical insight: **personal profiles generate 8x more engagement than company pages.** All three hosts should post from their personal accounts, each focusing on a different content pillar — one on AI research, one on practical dev tools, and one on industry news and opinions. Cross-engage by commenting on each other's posts with genuine, substantive comments.

Post **3–5 times per week** with a mix of formats: native video clips with captions (5x more engagement than static posts), carousel slides breaking down episode takeaways, text posts with strong hooks and hot takes on AI news, and polls like "Which LLM do you use most for coding?" **Never put external links in the post body** — LinkedIn suppresses posts with outbound links by ~40%. Instead, write native content and add the podcast link as the first comment.

Launch a **LinkedIn Newsletter** once each host has 1,000+ connections. LinkedIn sends triple notifications (email, push, in-app) to all connections and followers when you publish, bypassing the algorithm entirely. Average open rates hit **40%**, and newsletters are Google-indexed for long-term discoverability.

**Twitter/X strategy (developer community):**

Post **at least 1–3 times daily**. Threads get **3x more engagement** than single tweets — use 4–8 tweet threads for episode takeaways, paper breakdowns, and "5 things we learned" formats. Single tweets work for hot takes on breaking AI news, memes, and quick reactions. The **"reply guy" strategy** is surprisingly effective: consistent, genuine, insightful replies on popular AI/dev accounts can boost visibility dramatically. Quote-tweet interesting AI content with your own take to position as a thought leader. Use 1–3 hashtags maximum (#AI, #LLM, #BuildInPublic, #DevTools). Pin a tweet linking to your best episode.

**Scheduling tools:**

| Tool | Cost | Best for |
|------|------|----------|
| **Buffer** | Free (3 channels, 10 posts each) | Starting out — covers Twitter + LinkedIn + 1 more |
| **Typefully** | $8–12.50/mo | Twitter threads + LinkedIn posts; purpose-built for thought leadership |
| **Publer** | $4–12/mo | Cheapest paid option with bulk scheduling |

Start with **Buffer Free**. Upgrade to **Typefully Starter ($8/mo)** when ready to invest — its thread composition tools are particularly strong for developer content.

## 7. Start the newsletter now — you already have enough listeners

Multiple podcast marketing experts consistently recommend starting a newsletter as early as possible, regardless of audience size. Unlike social media where posts reach a fraction of followers, **emails land directly in inboxes** with 20–40% open rates. The newsletter becomes the bridge between casual listener and loyal subscriber.

**Platform recommendation: Beehiiv (free tier).** It supports up to **2,500 subscribers** for free, includes built-in growth tools (referral programs, cross-promotion recommendations), has no revenue cut on paid subscriptions, and offers a blog feature that creates a secondary web presence. The runner-up is Substack (unlimited free subscribers, strong network discovery among tech readers, built-in podcast hosting, but takes 10% of paid subscription revenue).

**What to include beyond episode summaries:**

The newsletter should deliver standalone value so subscribers open it even if they've already listened. Include 3–5 curated AI/dev news links (modeled after TLDR), one key insight or hot take from the episode (not the full summary — tease value), a "tool/repo of the week" spotlight, code snippets or techniques discussed, behind-the-scenes content about what the hosts are building, and a reader Q&A or community spotlight section.

**Publish weekly, on the same day as the episode release.** Tuesday is often cited as the best day for tech newsletter engagement. The newsletter and episode release should reinforce each other — subscribers see the newsletter, listen to the episode, and the habit loop strengthens.

**Growing the subscriber list:** Add a verbal CTA in every episode ("Sign up at adipod.ai for the links and tools we mentioned — plus bonus content we couldn't fit in the show"). Create a **lead magnet** — an "AI Developer Tools Cheat Sheet 2026" or "LLM Prompting Guide for Developers" — and gate it behind email signup. Place signup forms on every episode page and social media bio. Use Beehiiv's recommendation network to cross-promote with similar newsletters.

**Efficient creation workflow (30–45 minutes per issue):** Feed the episode transcript into Castmagic or Claude → get a draft with key takeaways, a compelling quote, and a 2-sentence summary → add 2–3 curated links from the week → compose in Beehiiv template → schedule send.

## 8. Convert viewers and followers into loyal subscribers

The core challenge: someone watches a YouTube clip or sees a LinkedIn post — how do you get them into a podcast app where they'll listen weekly? The answer is **specific, benefit-driven CTAs placed at moments of peak value delivery.**

**YouTube-to-podcast conversion:** The most effective CTA placement is **mid-video, immediately after delivering a valuable insight**: "If you want more of this every week, subscribe to Artificial Developer Intelligence in your favorite podcast app." Don't just say "subscribe" — say *why* ("so you never miss our weekly AI paper breakdowns"). Use curiosity gaps: "We dive way deeper into this on the podcast — we covered three more techniques we couldn't fit in this video." Also add the CTA in the pinned comment, the first two lines of the description (above the fold), and as an end screen element.

**Social media-to-podcast conversion:** On Twitter, share key takeaways as a thread and make the final tweet a CTA to listen. On LinkedIn, write a thoughtful post on the episode topic and end with "I discussed this in depth with my co-hosts on ADI — link in comments." Always post podcast links in comments on LinkedIn, not in the post body. Use **Linktree** or a custom landing page as your link-in-bio with the latest episode at the top, all podcast app links, and newsletter signup.

**Listener retention tactics:** ADI's recurring "AI Bubble Tracker" segment is excellent — recurring segments create habit and anticipation. Add more signature segments like "Paper of the Week," "Tool Spotlight," or "Listener Questions." **Launch a Discord server** for listeners (ideal for a tech audience) with channels for each recurring segment, general AI discussion, and show feedback. Start small — personally invite the first 50 listeners. Use Discord to crowdsource listener Q&A segments, creating a feedback loop that makes listeners feel ownership.

**Podcast app optimization:** Ask verbally for Apple Podcasts ratings: "A 5-star rating helps us reach more developers like you — it genuinely means a lot." Enable **Spotify polls and Q&A** on every episode (free, built-in). Listeners who interact with a show on Spotify are **4x more likely to return within 30 days**. Upload video versions to Spotify — Spotify heavily favors video podcasts in discovery algorithms.

## 9. How to spend $50/month on paid promotion

At this budget level, most paid channels offer too little volume to matter. The one exception is **Overcast ads** — the only paid channel where 100% of viewers are active podcast listeners inside a podcast app, one tap away from subscribing.

**The best strategy: save $50/month for 3 months, then buy one Overcast ad slot (~$130–$250 in the Technology category).** Case studies show costs of **$1.34–$2.98 per subscriber** with 40–80+ new subscribers per campaign. This is the single highest-quality paid traffic source for podcasts.

**What doesn't work at $50/month:**

- **Spotify podcast ads:** Minimum spend is $250 per campaign. Skip for now
- **Reddit ads:** Minimum daily budget is $5. At $50/month you'd get ~15–25 clicks — not enough data to optimize. Organic Reddit participation is far more effective
- **LinkedIn ads:** Recommended minimum is $1,000/month. Not feasible
- **Twitter/X ads:** CPC of $0.26–$0.50 gets you 100–200 clicks, but conversion to podcast listeners is historically poor
- **YouTube ads:** Possible at $50/month (167–500 views at $0.10–$0.30 CPV), but marginal. YouTube Shorts provide free algorithmic distribution that's more effective

**Alternative paid options worth considering:** **Podbay** offers a single-day ad for $25 — cheap enough to test. Free newsletter sponsorship swaps (find 5–10 AI/dev newsletters with similar audience sizes and propose mutual mentions) cost nothing and can be highly effective. **Goodpods** (free social podcast app) and **Podchaser** (free, claim your profile, encourage reviews) provide free visibility among podcast enthusiasts.

**Bottom line:** At $50/month, **90%+ of growth will come from free activities** — cross-promotion swaps, guest appearances, short-form clips, and community engagement. The paid budget is best concentrated into periodic Overcast buys rather than diluted across channels.

## What actually moves the needle for developer podcasts

Most podcast marketing advice is generic. For a technical developer audience, a few things matter disproportionately more than everything else. **Cross-promotion with similar podcasts is the single highest-ROI activity** — it's free, targets verified podcast listeners, and leverages existing trust. Second is **YouTube Shorts as a discovery engine** — the algorithm rapidly tests short content across new audiences, and tech CPMs run $12–28 (strong future monetization potential). Third is **the newsletter as a retention flywheel** — it's the only channel where you fully own the audience relationship.

The biggest mistake would be trying to do everything at once. The roadmap above sequences activities so each builds on the last. Weeks 1–4 focus entirely on building the content multiplication system. Only after that engine runs smoothly do you layer on outreach, cross-promotion, and community building. Paid promotion comes last because at 100 downloads, every dollar spent on ads would be more efficiently invested in tools that multiply free content.

ADI's distinctive advantage — three hosts with comedic chemistry covering a serious topic — is exactly what cuts through in developer communities where dry, corporate content dominates. Lean into the personality. The memes, hot takes, and self-deprecating humor that feel natural to the show are precisely what performs on Twitter, Reddit, and YouTube Shorts. Technical depth earns respect; personality earns shares.
